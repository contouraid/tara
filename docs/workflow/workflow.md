# 10: Integration of AI into Clinical Workflow

Clinical integration is the work required to turn a validated model into a dependable service. It includes process design, interfaces, responsibilities, training, change management, monitoring, and retirement. A model can be technically excellent and still fail because it delivers the wrong output, at the wrong time, to the wrong person, without a safe way to disagree.

Radiation oncology is especially sensitive to integration quality. Each treatment depends on a chain of images, structures, prescriptions, plans, approvals, machine parameters, and delivery records. AI should be introduced as a controlled change to that chain, with measurable benefit and explicit ownership.

## Begin with the Clinical Problem

The first question is not "Where can AI be added?" It is "Which clinical problem should be solved?" A useful problem statement describes the current process, who experiences the burden or risk, its frequency and consequence, and the desired outcome.

Examples include:

- reducing the time from simulation to first treatment;
- decreasing major contour corrections;
- finding plan or chart errors earlier;
- reducing variation in plan quality;
- selecting patients who need adaptive review;
- eliminating duplicate data entry;
- improving access to specialist review.

An automation target chosen only because data are available may optimize a low-value step while leaving the actual bottleneck unchanged.

## Map the Existing Workflow

### Define Scope and Boundaries

Choose a start and end point, such as image acquisition to contour approval or plan approval to first fraction. Identify every role, system, handoff, queue, and decision inside that boundary.

A useful current-state map records:

- task sequence and parallel work;
- responsible and accountable roles;
- required inputs and produced outputs;
- software and data interfaces;
- average and tail completion times;
- rework and exception paths;
- safety checks and approvals;
- downtime procedures.

The exception path is often more important than the ideal path. Complex cases, missing images, machine changes, and urgent treatments are where brittle automation becomes visible.

### Measure Before Changing

Establish a baseline before deployment. Depending on the problem, measures may include turnaround time, active staff time, queue time, number of handoffs, correction rate, incident reports, cancellation rate, plan-quality measures, or patient delay.

Average time alone can hide operational harm. Report medians and tail behavior, such as the 90th percentile, because a small group of severely delayed patients may matter more than a modest average improvement.

### Find Constraints and Failure Demand

A local efficiency gain does not guarantee a faster complete pathway. Faster auto-contouring may simply move the queue to physician review. AI may also create **failure demand**: work generated by checking, correcting, rerunning, or explaining automation.

Observe real work, not only written policy. Staff frequently use informal communication and compensating steps that are absent from a formal process map but essential to safe operation.

## Select the Integration Point

### Decision Support, Drafting, and Automation

AI roles form a spectrum:

- **Background analysis:** computes a signal without interrupting routine work;
- **Prioritization:** changes the order in which cases are reviewed;
- **Draft generation:** creates a contour, plan, or text for mandatory review;
- **Decision support:** presents a recommendation and evidence;
- **Conditional automation:** acts automatically within defined criteria and escalates exceptions;
- **Autonomous action:** acts without case-level human approval.

Risk generally rises with autonomy and irreversibility. The evidence, safeguards, and monitoring should rise with it. Draft generation is not automatically low risk: a plausible draft can anchor reviewers and propagate unnoticed errors.

### Place the Output Where Work Happens

Users should not need to monitor a separate dashboard or copy results manually when a safe interface can deliver the output inside the treatment planning system, oncology information system, or review environment.

The interface should show:

- patient and study identity;
- model output and units;
- model/version provenance;
- completion and data timestamps;
- applicability limits and warnings;
- status: draft, reviewed, approved, or rejected;
- access to relevant source data;
- the expected user action.

Manual transcription and ambiguous status labels create avoidable risk.

### Preserve Accountability

For every output, define who reviews it, who may edit it, who approves it, and who owns the system. "The team" is not a role. A RACI matrix—responsible, accountable, consulted, informed—can clarify ownership across clinicians, physicists, therapists, informatics, IT, cybersecurity, vendors, and governance groups.

## Technical Integration

### Data Interfaces

Radiotherapy systems exchange DICOM images and DICOM-RT objects, but implementation details vary. Integration testing should verify patient identity, frame of reference, orientation, units, structure names, dose scaling, transfer completeness, and behavior with unsupported objects.

APIs and messaging interfaces should use authenticated connections, least-privilege access, encryption, audit logs, timeouts, and explicit error responses. A partial transfer must not be displayed as a completed result.

### Orchestration and State

An AI service needs more than inference code. It needs job submission, queue management, input validation, retries, status tracking, result storage, provenance, and failure notification. Idempotent processing prevents duplicate outputs when a job is retried.

Clinical state must be explicit. A result generated from an earlier contour or image should become stale when its input changes. The workflow should prevent approval of an output whose provenance no longer matches the current case.

### Reliability and Downtime

Define availability and latency requirements from the clinical pathway. An offline research pipeline may tolerate hours; online adaptive radiotherapy may require minutes. Monitor service health, but distinguish technical availability from clinically usable output.

A downtime plan should answer:

- How do users recognize an outage or delayed result?
- Does work continue manually, wait, or move to another system?
- Who is contacted and after what interval?
- How are queued cases reconciled after recovery?
- How is a rollback performed after a faulty release?

The manual pathway must remain practiced if it is the fallback.

## Design the Future-State Workflow

### Standard Path

Document the complete future path from trigger to archived decision. Include automatic and human steps, time expectations, notifications, review displays, approval, export, and downstream checks.

For AI-generated contours, for example:

1. a verified planning image triggers the model;
2. input rules confirm site, modality, orientation, and completeness;
3. the model generates versioned draft structures;
4. the clinician reviews images and every relevant boundary;
5. edits and approval are recorded;
6. an independent plan-stage check verifies expected structures;
7. monitoring records runtime, edits, failures, and overrides.

### Exception Path

Define what happens for unsupported anatomy, prior surgery, implants, severe artifacts, pediatric patients, incomplete scans, model abstention, and unavailable service. Exceptions should reach an appropriate human queue rather than silently defaulting to a normal result.

### Independent Safety Checks

Do not remove an established check merely because AI performs a related task. First determine whether the new and old checks detect the same failures and whether they share dependencies. A new workflow may eventually retire low-value duplication, but only after monitored evidence and formal risk review.

## Local Evaluation and Phased Deployment

### Technical and Silent Phases

Begin with integration testing on nonclinical or retrospective cases. Then use silent deployment on current cases, where outputs are captured but do not influence care. Silent operation reveals data quality, latency, prevalence, and integration failures in the real environment.

### Limited Pilot

A pilot should have a narrow site, trained users, explicit inclusion criteria, heightened monitoring, and a defined end date. Prespecify success, pause, and stop criteria. Collect both intended benefits and unintended burdens.

### Controlled Scale-Up

Expand one dimension at a time: more users, disease sites, scanners, or automation. Each expansion should pass a readiness review. Broad rollout immediately after a small successful pilot makes it difficult to identify which change caused a problem.

[Chapter 9](../validation/validation.md) describes technical, silent, reader, prospective, and post-deployment evaluation in more detail.

## User Training and Competency

### Train for the Task, Not the Algorithm

Users do not need to reproduce the model architecture. They do need to know:

- intended use and excluded cases;
- required inputs and expected outputs;
- common and high-consequence failure modes;
- how to review, edit, reject, and escalate;
- how to recognize stale or incomplete results;
- how to use the downtime workflow;
- who remains accountable for the decision.

Training should use realistic cases, including failures. Demonstrating only typical successful cases teaches trust without teaching safe skepticism.

### Assess Competency

Attendance is not competency. Use case-based exercises, observed workflow, or short assessments. Record completion and reassess after material system changes or long periods without use.

Different roles need different depth. A clinician reviewer, IT operator, physicist commissioning the system, and governance lead have distinct responsibilities.

### Provide Support at Go-Live

At go-live, provide clear support channels, rapid triage, and local champions across professions and shifts. Capture questions and near misses. Update procedures and training when recurring confusion appears.

## Clinical Acceptance and Trust

Trust should be calibrated, not maximized. Too little trust prevents useful adoption; too much produces automation bias. Users build appropriate trust when the system is predictable, limitations are visible, feedback is acknowledged, and governance responds to failures.

### Involve Stakeholders Early

Include the people who perform and receive the work: radiation oncologists, physicists, dosimetrists, therapists, nurses, administrative staff, informatics teams, IT, and patients where relevant. Early participation identifies hidden work and improves the legitimacy of design decisions.

### Explain the Evidence

Share local validation results, failure examples, subgroup limitations, expected benefit, and monitoring plan. Avoid presenting one aggregate accuracy number as proof of safety. Where explanations or uncertainty are displayed, teach users what they mean and what they do not mean.

### Make Feedback Actionable

Provide a low-friction method to report wrong outputs, workflow friction, and safety concerns. Link reports to model version and input provenance. Close the loop by communicating the investigation and any resulting change.

## Change Management

### Establish the Case for Change

People assess change through its effect on patient care, professional responsibility, workload, and identity. State the problem, expected benefit, evidence, and constraints honestly. Automation that saves one group's time while adding unmeasured work to another will face justified resistance.

### Align Policy, Procedure, and Incentives

Update standard operating procedures, role descriptions, on-call coverage, audit requirements, and governance records. If staff are expected to review AI carefully but are scheduled as if review takes no time, the process design is internally inconsistent.

### Manage Versions as Clinical Changes

A model update is a clinical change, even if the user interface is unchanged. Use change control to document the reason, affected components, validation, training impact, release date, rollback, and monitoring period.

### Learn from Normal Work and Incidents

Review edit patterns, overrides, downtime, near misses, and incidents. Avoid interpreting every override as user error or every accepted output as model success. Qualitative investigation often reveals issues that aggregate metrics miss.

## Governance

An oversight group should have authority to approve deployment, restrict use, require corrective action, and suspend the system. Its review should cover:

- clinical ownership and intended use;
- evidence and residual risks;
- privacy, security, and data governance;
- equity and subgroup performance;
- vendor and update management;
- incident response and regulatory reporting;
- monitoring and periodic reapproval;
- retirement and data retention.

WHO guidance emphasizes autonomy, safety, transparency, accountability, inclusiveness, and responsiveness throughout AI design and use [[1]](https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/9789240029200). These principles become operational only when connected to named owners, measures, and decision rights.

## Measuring Success

Use a balanced set of measures:

### Clinical Quality and Safety

- clinically significant error and correction rates;
- protocol adherence and plan-quality measures;
- incidents, near misses, and severity;
- subgroup outcomes and access.

### Workflow

- total turnaround and tail latency;
- active time by role;
- queue length and handoffs;
- rework, overrides, and abstentions;
- downtime and recovery.

### Human Factors

- usability and workload;
- reviewer agreement and vigilance;
- training competency;
- appropriate versus inappropriate reliance.

### Economic and Operational Value

- implementation and maintenance cost;
- released or shifted capacity;
- cost per successfully completed case;
- sustainability of support and monitoring.

Measure the full pathway. A model that shortens one task but increases downstream correction has not created the claimed value.

## Current Research and Recent Advances

- **Readiness and workforce capacity:** WHO's 2025 assessment across 50 European-region Member States highlights governance, workforce readiness, data governance, and stakeholder engagement as health-system prerequisites for AI integration [[2]](https://www.who.int/europe/publications/i/item/WHO-EURO-2025-12707-52481-81028). _(added: 2026-07)_
- **Clinical adoption is uneven across tasks:** A 2026 national survey of Italian radiotherapy departments reported more routine use of autosegmentation than autoplanning, illustrating that integration maturity depends on use case, infrastructure, and workflow—not AI availability alone [[3]](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ejmp.2026.105867). _(added: 2026-07)_
- **Human-centered governance:** Current guidance continues to emphasize human autonomy, transparent limitations, accountability, and continuous evaluation in actual use. These are workflow design requirements, not abstract ethical additions [[1]](https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/9789240029200). _(added: 2026-07)_

## Recap

Successful AI integration starts with a measured clinical problem and a map of real work. The future workflow must define triggers, interfaces, provenance, review, exceptions, downtime, ownership, and independent safety checks. Phased deployment, role-specific competency, calibrated trust, change control, and balanced monitoring turn a model into a clinical service. The relevant unit of evaluation is the complete human-AI system and its effect on patients and staff.
